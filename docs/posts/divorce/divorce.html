Divorce

 God made an amazing union in His institution of marriage, and allows us to give glory to Him by participating in it ourselves. Christ has His own bride in His body - the church. God was married to Israel in the old Testament, and Israel was to act as His body, with God as the head of this body. However, Israel was divorced from God for worshipping other gods. Idol worship by Israel, since she was married to God, was God’s wife committing adultery against Him. There’s not much detail given about Israel’s actions, but there is a specific occurrence noted here:

“And he received them at their hand, and fashioned it with a graving tool, after he had made it a molten calf: and they said, These be thy gods, O Israel, which brought thee up out of the land of Egypt.” (Exodus 32:4)

It seems that God had suffered adultery for a long time, through many occurrences, and that this adultery had been committed even since their time spent in Egypt. We can see that this occurred in Egypt from the following examples:

“Neither left she her whoredoms brought from Egypt: for in her youth they lay with her, and they bruised the breasts of her virginity, and poured their whoredom upon her.” (Ezekiel 23:8)

“And the Babylonians came to her into the bed of love, and they defiled her with their whoredom, and she was polluted with them, and her mind was alienated from them. So she discovered her whoredoms, and discovered her nakedness: then my mind was alienated from her, like as my mind was alienated from her sister. Yet she multiplied her whoredoms, in calling to remembrance the days of her youth, wherein she had played the harlot in the land of Egypt.” (Ezekiel 23:17-19)

“Thus will I make thy lewdness to cease from thee, and thy whoredom brought from the land of Egypt: so that thou shalt not lift up thine eyes unto them, nor remember Egypt any more.” (Ezekiel 23:27)

And, we see God’s divorce here:

“And I saw, when for all the causes whereby backsliding Israel committed adultery I had put her away, and given her a bill of divorce; yet her treacherous sister Judah feared not, but went and played the harlot also.” (Jeremiah 3:8)

 God describes the wicked heart of Israel in great detail, showing Israel as His wife who had made herself a prostitute for other men on every hill, and under every tree1 2, and as one so brazen and unashamed3 that she wouldn’t even blush about it; Israel had no embarrassment for her whoredom with many men. She sought after other men as a donkey in heat would4, lusting after their large genitals and horse-like emissions5, and giving her breasts to them6 as if they did not already belong to another. She didn’t even wish for these men to pursue her before giving herself to them in adultery4, and even chased them herself until her shoes had worn holes in them, and her throat was dry from thirst7.

 Hopefully this description helps us understand the absolute depravity and long-term unrepentance that God suffered before divorcing Israel. This was not just one occurrence of adultery that He divorced because of, but many occurrences. It was also after many repeated refusals by His wife to return to Him. In sum, these actions of God show that even though divorce is possible for adultery, God wills that marriages be restored in spite of it, if the offender confesses and repents. If God suffered Israel this much, we should be willing to reflect this image before ending a marriage, and it appears that only after unrepentant adultery should divorce be used.

Define divorce

 The entire discussion of marriage, divorce, and remarriage - especially when discussing details in the Word - often becomes muddled by differing definitions. Miscommunications are also more common for complex ideas and scenarios.

 A somewhat common believe is that divorce inherently breaks a marriage on its own and justifies any remarriage. However, this belief is not universal and causes misunderstandings. 1. One person may be suggesting that a particular issue justifies divorce, yet while they have in mind that divorce for this particular issue only allows for separation (with reunion being required after confession and repentance), the listener may take it to mean that both divorce and remarriage are justified. 2. When one attempts to give examples of divorce - without making a claim about whether that example is of a justified divorce - some may take the example as describing a justified divorce.

Problem 2 may be compounded with problem 1: In this scenario, if I say, for example, that divorce for life-threatening physical abuse is justified, I may intend for it to mean that the abused spouse can separate with the understanding that they’ll rejoin their spouse when confessed and repented to, and that remarriage isn’t an option during the separation period; however, a given listener may take it to mean that physical abuse justifies divorce, and then apply it along with their presumption that all divorce justifies remarriage to conclude that abuse justifies remarriage after divorce. Perhaps the context of the abuse being physical at all is lost on them at some point, and then they take the saying to mean that any kind of abuse justifies divorce and remarriage.

 Take for example the discussion of remarriage. I may question for a given scenario whether someone can remarry after a divorce. Someone may be confused about whether remarriage is referring to rejoining to the spouse who was divorced, or marrying someone else, and believe I’m referring to one when I’m speaking about the other.

 Take for example discussions of adultery. If someone says simply, “Adultery is sex outside of marriage.”, one may take it to mean what was intended (which was that adultery is a sexual act conducted outside of the bounds of an existing marriage), while another may take the saying to mean “Adultery includes sex before marriage” (since that could count as being “outside” of a marriage). Then they may consider fornication to be a subset of adultery rather than vice versa.

  Hopefully the previous statements give a clear image of just how convoluted the entire discussion may get. However, I’d like to attempt to define divorce, share what the Word says about it, share what I believe the Word is saying, and give some sources that I hope are helpful in bringing understanding to the topic.

 As a short definition, I’ll say divorce is simply one spouse declaring the dissolution of a marriage, followed by either spouse physically leaving the relationship and refusing to live with the other.

There are several questions this differing of definitions brings up: - Does divorce mean the complete dissolution of the marriage, in the sense that remarriage is justifiable? - Does divorce only mean separation, as a temporary ending of the relationship which must be restored if possible? - Is divorce for a reason that doesn’t justify remarriage justifiable? Is it permissible - as in, can the church permit it? - Could divorce be either of the first two bullets depending on the situation? - The Word says adultery or abandonment by an unbeliever justify remarriage. - Divorces that don’t justify remarriage have happened, and supposing that remarriage hasn’t happened, those marriages should be restored. - Could divorce be both of first two bullets, supposing that remarriage (to a different spouse) is justifiable in a situation where the original marriage could be restored? - Must a marriage that can be restored be restored? This question could be taken 2 different ways. 1. If confession and repentance don’t matter, then perhaps it’s technically always the case that marriage can and should be restored. 2. Is it mandatory to restore a marriage after a divorce when the offended party has been confessed and repented to?

Someone who divorced, whether it was justifiable or not, may not have divorced for a reason that justified remarriage. Another who divorced may have suffered adultery from someone who refuses to confess and repent, which does appear to justify remarriage after divorce.

Justifications for divorce

Does failure to fulfill duties justify divorce?

 It’s commonly said that in the old testament, both husband and wife have duties to each other, and that if they don’t provide sustenance, clothing, and conjugal (sexual) rights to each other - generally with the husband working, within the world, to provide the raw cloth and food materials, and the woman providing the refined products, within the home, of clothing items and prepared meals - then the offended party has the right to divorce. The passage often cited in this is:

“And if a man sell his daughter to be a maidservant, she shall not go out as the menservants do. If she please not her master, who hath betrothed her to himself, then shall he let her be redeemed: to sell her unto a strange nation he shall have no power, seeing he hath dealt deceitfully with her. And if he have betrothed her unto his son, he shall deal with her after the manner of daughters. If he take him another wife; her food, her raiment, and her duty of marriage, shall he not diminish. And if he do not these three unto her, then shall she go out free without money.” (Exodus 21:7-11)

Justifications for divorce and remarriage

 I’m more confident in the argument coming from Matthew 19:98. As a short definition, I’ll say divorce is simply one spouse declaring the dissolution of a marriage, followed by either spouse physically leaving the relationship of and refusing to live with the other. Some believe that this dissolution of the marriage frees them to marry another. However, this verse makes it clear that divorce does not in itself allow remarriage.

“And I say unto you, Whosoever shall put away his wife, except it be for fornication, and shall marry another, committeth adultery: and whoso marrieth her which is put away doth commit adultery.” (Matthew 19:9)

The KJV translation uses the words fornication and adultery here. We generally consider fornication to be engaging in a sexual act before marriage, and adultery to be engaging in a sexual act with someone other than one’s spouse after marriage. However, this verse isn’t saying that someone can divorce their spouse if they find out that they engaged in a sexual act before they were married to them. The word translated into fornication is the Greek word “Porneia”. It often believed that Jesus used this word to refer to every form of fornication, including things such as incest and beastiality, rather than just adultery.

Even if divorce is justified because of unrepentant adultery, I’d wonder, because of God’s plan to redeem and remarry Israel, whether we should really ever divorce and remarry a different person than one’s previous spouse.

 Does hate count as literal murder? >“Whosoever hateth his brother is a murderer: and ye know that no murderer hath eternal life abiding in him.” (1 John 3:15)

As with many parts of the Word, this is interpreted in accordance with of the rest of scripture. So, this first isn’t to be interpreted as saying that if you hate, you’ve physically committed murder, or that someone who has murdered cannot is disqualified from eternal life altogether. The murderer who has no eternal life abiding in him is in the present tense, meaning one who has not put their faith in God for their redemption and repented of their sin cannot have eternal life within them. If a murderer continues with a murderous heart, without confession and repentance, they’ve not accepted God as their redeemer, putting away the former self. Interpret this along with:

“Ye have heard that it was said by them of old time, Thou shalt not commit adultery: But I say unto you, That whosoever looketh on a woman to lust after her hath committed adultery with her already in his heart.” (Matthew 5:27-28).

This shows us that it is not only the act of sin which condemns us, but a heart which wills toward a sin. Human’s cannot know one’s heart, so our judges convict based on actions and evidence. However, God knows our hearts, and can judge us for holding evil in them. That is why the word also says:

“Therefore to him that knoweth to do good, and doeth it not, to him it is sin.” (James 4:17)

These verses lead us to understand that even our heart is sinful, and not only our acts, or lack thereof.

So, we should recognize that lust does not mean that one has literally committed fornication or adultery. The question is then whether it’s a sin to hold things such as hatred or lust in your heart.

If you pay attention, the verse specifically addresses adultery, which is only one engaging in a sexual act outside of an existing marriage, and does not address fornication. I believe we’d have to conclude that lust for an unmarried woman is also sin, since fornication is also a sin. So, if you continue to hold hatred or lust in your heart, it’s a sin for you. The point is that we’re all still in fallen flesh, and we must continue to confess and repent to God and look to his grace and mercy.

To answer the original question, we must finally ask whether sin justifies divorce, since we know we can classify lust as sin.
